Research Overview

Dr. Tremblay's research develops computational and machine learning methods for problems in molecular biology and genomics. His lab works at the boundary between algorithms, statistics, and biological data, with the goal of building tools that help biologists extract meaning from high-throughput experimental data.

Current projects span three areas: (1) single-cell RNA sequencing analysis, including cell type annotation, trajectory inference, and gene regulatory network reconstruction; (2) protein structure prediction and protein-ligand docking using deep learning; and (3) multi-omics data integration, combining genomic, transcriptomic, and proteomic measurements to model disease heterogeneity.

On the methods side, the lab is particularly interested in transformer architectures for biological sequences, self-supervised pretraining on large genomic corpora, and causal inference approaches for understanding perturbation effects in CRISPR screens.

Research Areas

  • Single-cell genomics and transcriptomics
  • Deep learning for protein structure and function
  • Gene regulatory network inference
  • Self-supervised learning on biological sequences
  • Multi-omics data integration
  • Causal inference for CRISPR perturbation screens

Lab Members

The Tremblay Computational Biology Lab currently has three Ph.D. students, one postdoc, and two undergraduate research assistants. We welcome applications from students interested in computational biology, bioinformatics, and machine learning for life sciences. Undergraduate students may apply for summer NSERC USRA positions by emailing a CV, unofficial transcript, and a one-paragraph statement of interest to Dr. Tremblay. Preferred background: Python, statistics, some exposure to biology or bioinformatics.
