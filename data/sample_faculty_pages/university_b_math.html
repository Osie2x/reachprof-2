Research

My work sits at the intersection of harmonic analysis, operator theory, and their applications to signal processing and data science. I am particularly interested in frame theory, where I study how overcomplete systems of vectors can be used to represent and reconstruct signals robustly in the presence of noise or erasure. A secondary thread of my research concerns the spectral theory of random matrices and its applications to high-dimensional statistical estimation.

Over the past several years I have been developing connections between frame-theoretic methods and problems in compressed sensing and sparse recovery. This work draws on tools from convex optimization, probabilistic combinatorics, and finite-dimensional functional analysis. I am also interested in how these ideas extend to manifold-valued data and non-Euclidean settings.

A more recent interest is in applied harmonic analysis for graph-structured data, specifically graph signal processing: defining Fourier transforms and wavelet transforms on graphs, and understanding what sampling theory looks like in that setting. This connects naturally to spectral graph theory and to questions about graph neural network expressiveness.

Students

I welcome inquiries from motivated students at all levels. Prospective Ph.D. and Master's students should apply through the graduate admissions process and mention my name. Undergraduate students interested in NSERC USRA positions or independent study courses are welcome to email me directly with a transcript and a brief description of what they have studied so far. Strong preparation in real analysis and linear algebra is expected.
